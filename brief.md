## Question

When a firm grows after hiring, who made the growth? The question is a credit-assignment problem, the same one reinforcement learning has carried since its founding, and cooperative game theory solved its allocative core in 1953 with the Shapley value. What does political economy look like if that solution is taken seriously at the moment machine intelligence becomes a production factor rather than a tool?

## Claim

Contribution to a jointly produced surplus is the Shapley value: the unique division consistent with efficiency, symmetry, the dummy axiom, and additivity, equal to each factor's marginal contribution averaged over every order of assembly. Three exact games carry the argument. A minimal firm shows naive growth-per-head overstating new hires more than fourfold and isolates a three-way synergy dividend that belongs to no participant alone. A task-continuum production game over intelligence, compute, energy, and human labor, solved exactly along the whole automation path, shows total output multiplying sixfold while labor's share of attributed value collapses from all of it to an eighth, and yields two structural facts: intelligence and its essential complements are forced to equal Shapley values by symmetry (attribution is blind to which is the clever one), and intelligence's share of value is capped at one-third by two essential complements, at 1/(k+1) by k, for any finite capability. The Shapley singularity (intelligence's share approaching one) is therefore an ownership event, reachable only by absorbing or shedding complements, not an intelligence event. A verification pair locates the last human wage in certification and exhibits the gap between the Shapley point (determinate contribution) and the core (indeterminate appropriation), which is where distributional politics lives. The frame is bounded: a Shapley value is an audit under a chosen value function, not a desert claim.

## Cornerstone literature

- Shapley (1953), the value; Young (1988), the marginality axiomatization; Harsanyi (1963), dividends; von Neumann & Morgenstern (1944), the characteristic function — the formal core.
- Smith (1776), Ricardo (1817), Marx (1867) — classical political economy and the synergy it could not divide; Clark (1899), Wicksteed (1894), the marginal-product adding-up problem; Alchian & Demsetz (1972), Holmström (1982), team production and its impossibility; Milgrom & Roberts (1990), supermodularity.
- Roemer (1982), Cohen (1995), Reiff (2013), von Platz (2021), Elster (1985), Wood (1989) — the analytical-Marxist approach to and refusal of cooperative game theory.
- Minsky (1961), Sutton & Barto (2018), Wolpert & Tumer (2001), Foerster et al. (2018), Lundberg & Lee (2017) — credit assignment in machine learning, the engineering rediscovery of the Shapley integrand.
- Aghion, Jones & Jones (2019), Acemoglu & Restrepo (2019), Bresnahan & Trajtenberg (1995), Brynjolfsson, Rock & Syverson (2021), Nordhaus (2021), Korinek & Stiglitz (2019) — AI, growth, automation, and the singularity tests.
- Pearl (2009), causal value functions; Goodhart (1975), Strathern (1997), the index-gaming limit; Baumol (1967), cost disease; Hayek (1945), prices as bargaining not contribution.
